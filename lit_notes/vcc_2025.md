# Virtual Cell Challenge 2025 (Cell commentary)

**Venue:** Cell. doi:10.1016/j.cell.2025.06.008. Published 26 June 2025. Leading Edge Commentary, "Virtual Cell Challenge: Toward a Turing test for the virtual cell." URL: https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(25)00675-0. Companion wrap-up: Arc Institute, "Virtual Cell Challenge 2025 Wrap-Up: Winners and Reflections," 6 December 2025, https://arcinstitute.org/news/virtual-cell-challenge-2025-wrap-up, presented at NeurIPS 2025.

## Claim
The Cell commentary announces the inaugural Virtual Cell Challenge (VCC), an Arc Institute benchmark competition for predicting single-cell transcriptomic responses to genetic perturbation in a held-out cell type. The 6 December 2025 NeurIPS wrap-up reports that "perturbation prediction models are not yet consistently outperforming naive baselines across all metrics," that "purely AI-based approaches did not consistently outperform statistical baselines on DES and MAE," and that "Almost all models performed worse than baseline on MAE." The wrap-up explicitly frames future work around metric design, distributional fidelity, and robustness rather than raw scaling, which is the gap ConfPert targets.

## Method
VCC is a recurring open benchmark hosted at virtualcellchallenge.org. The 2025 task is "context generalization": predict effects of single-gene CRISPRi perturbations in a held-out cell type, H1 human embryonic stem cells (H1 hESCs). Participants are given partial Challenge data plus permitted public Perturb-seq corpora; they submit predicted gene expression vectors per perturbation per cell. Submissions are scored on three primary metrics (PDS, DES, MAE) plus four supplementary metrics for the Generalist Prize (Pearson delta, Spearman log-fold change, AUPRC, Spearman effect size), all computed via Arc's Cell-Eval suite. Winning models are hybrid: deep encoders combined with classical statistical features (training-set DEG frequency, mean expression, Wilcoxon summaries, pseudo-bulk references).

## Datasets / experiments
Purpose-built dataset of approximately 300,000 single-cell RNA-seq profiles from H1 hESCs spanning 300 CRISPRi target genes. Dual-guide CRISPRi (dCas9-KRAB), 10x Genomics Flex chemistry, more than 50,000 UMIs per cell, around 1,000 cells per perturbation, 63% of cells with both guides detected, 83% of cells with greater than 80% knockdown. Splits: 150 training perturbations, 50 validation, 100 test, stratified by DEG count and cell quality. Scale of participation per the wrap-up: 5,000+ registrants from 114 countries, 1,200+ teams submitting, 300+ teams with final submissions. State (Arc's own model) is the official baseline, alongside training-set mean and naive baselines. Permitted external training data includes Tahoe-100M, K562/A375 Perturb-seq, and PerturbAtlas.

## Metrics
- **PDS (Perturbation Discrimination Score):** for each test perturbation t, count fraction of other perturbations p whose ground truth is closer (Manhattan / L1) to the prediction than the true target; normalized as PDiscNorm = 1 - 2*PDisc. Perfect = 0, random ~ 0.5. The wrap-up notes PDS is scale-sensitive: "As models increase prediction magnitude, PDS scores rise, asymptotically approaching sign-based cosine similarity."
- **DES (Differential Expression Score):** Wilcoxon rank-sum with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction; fraction of truly DE genes recovered in the predicted DE set. The wrap-up: "Evaluates whether models identify the correct set of upregulated and downregulated genes... biologists often view the set of differentially expressed genes as the most relevant output from Perturb-seq experiments."
- **MAE (Mean Absolute Error):** absolute error across roughly 18,000 genes between predicted and observed gene expression. The wrap-up: "MAE was designed to be maximally expressive and capture aspects that the other metrics may miss... much of the signal in raw counts is influenced by technical noise, stochastic dropout, and biological heterogeneity."
- **Generalist Prize metrics:** Pearson delta correlation, Spearman correlation of log-fold change, AUPRC on DE calls, Spearman correlation of effect size.

## What we steal for ConfPert
The wrap-up flags exactly the gap ConfPert fills: distributional fidelity, calibration of uncertainty, and robustness to metric trade-offs are explicitly identified as unsolved. Verbatim, load-bearing passages:

1. "Almost all models performed worse than baseline on MAE. While not entirely unexpected, this effectively excluded MAE from competitive optimization."
2. "Models can now inflate variance, distort distributions, or even submit nearly random predictions and still achieve excellent DE and PDS scores without being penalized for inaccuracy." (Substack analysis cited by wrap-up community discussion.)
3. "No single metric captures 'model quality', and we observed clear trade-offs where optimizing one metric sometimes came at the cost of others. We are studying this year's submissions to inform future metric refinements, potentially including norm-matched PDS variants and new biological relevance criteria."
4. "Purely AI-based approaches did not consistently outperform statistical baselines on DES and MAE."
5. "Pure end-to-end neural networks were yet to outperform hybrid models, indicating that pure scaling in size cannot reliably improve outcomes without integrating biological understanding into model design."
6. "There's much more to do beyond these first steps to close the gap between current models and biological fidelity."
7. The Generalist Prize winner (Altos Labs, "go-with-the-flow") "developed a flow matching generative model to infer heterogeneous cellular responses to perturbations" rather than predict response means, signaling that the field is moving from point predictions to full distributions, which is precisely where conformal prediction sets supply calibrated coverage guarantees.

ConfPert ingests these signals as: (a) wrap any VCC submission's per-cell prediction as a base predictor, (b) emit calibrated prediction sets for DE-gene identity and effect-size magnitude with finite-sample coverage, (c) report norm-matched, calibration-aware variants of PDS/DES/MAE that the VCC organizers explicitly flag as needed.

## What we wrap
Not a model. Positioning citation. ConfPert positions itself as the calibration layer for any VCC-eligible predictor (State, xTrimoSCPerturb, TransPert, go-with-the-flow, X), providing finite-sample coverage guarantees on top of the point predictions submitted to the leaderboard. The Cell-Eval suite (https://github.com/ArcInstitute/cell-eval) becomes the evaluation harness; ConfPert adds calibration and coverage diagnostics that Cell-Eval currently lacks.

## Failure modes / caveats
- MAE is gameable downward only by predicting the training mean; raw count noise dominates true signal at the per-gene level. Per the wrap-up: "the absolute value of MAE may be very difficult to lower beyond simply predicting the average cell state."
- PDS is scale-sensitive and rewards getting the sign of expression changes correct rather than exact magnitudes; community analysis published as ArXiv "Effects of Distance Metrics and Scaling on the Perturbation Discrimination Score" (Team Outlier).
- The capping of MAE penalties at zero produced the "evaluation collapse" discussed by gmdbioinformatics.substack.com: "Models can now inflate variance, distort distributions, or even submit nearly random predictions."
- Single cell type (H1 hESC), single perturbation modality (CRISPRi knockdown), single chemistry (10x Flex). No drug perturbations, no combinatorial perturbations, no protein/epigenetic readouts. Per a related 2025 perspective, "a comprehensive modeling of cellular behavior will require integrating diverse biological modalities including transcriptional, proteomic, and epigenetic across temporal and spatial dimensions."
- Top teams used pseudo-bulk rather than single-cell representations, so the Challenge effectively benchmarks mean-response prediction more than full-distribution prediction. This leaves heterogeneity-aware evaluation as an open problem that ConfPert addresses.
