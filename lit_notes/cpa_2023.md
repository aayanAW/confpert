# Lotfollahi et al. 2023 - CPA: Compositional Perturbation Autoencoder

**Venue:** Mol Syst Biol 2023. DOI 10.15252/msb.202211517. PMC10258562.

## Claim

CPA is a perturbation autoencoder that disentangles a cell's basal state from perturbation and covariate effects via adversarial covariate removal, enabling out-of-distribution prediction of perturbed expression for unseen drug-dose combinations, unseen cell types, and unseen drug pairs. The paper frames the goal as: "CPA learns to in silico predict transcriptional perturbation response at the single-cell level for unseen dosages, cell types, time points, and species" and that "CPA can predict unseen drug combinations while outperforming baseline models." Output is a counterfactual perturbed distribution per cell, returned as a mean (and variance for the Gaussian head), not as Monte Carlo samples by default.

## Method

CPA has three networks. An encoder maps gene expression x_i to a "basal" latent z_basal_i. Learnable additive embeddings for perturbation (drug, dose) and covariates (cell line, donor, species) are added to z_basal to form z_i. A decoder f_dec maps z_i to a parameterized output distribution over genes. An adversarial classifier on z_basal predicts perturbation and covariates; the encoder is trained to fool it so z_basal is purged of perturbation and covariate signal.

Decoder output. The implementation supports three reconstruction heads: `gauss`, `nb`, `zinb` (via `recon_loss`). For Gaussian, the decoder returns a mean and variance: "a decoder f_dec accepts the estimated perturbed state and returns f_mu_dec(z_i) and f_sigma2_dec(z_i), that is, the estimated mean and variance." For NB/ZINB the generative pass constructs a `NegativeBinomial` or `ZeroInflatedNegativeBinomial` distribution with mean parameter mu = px_rate.

Reconstruction loss (Gaussian form, paper notation):

l_i := log sigma(f_dec_sigma2(z_i))^2 + ||f_dec_mu(z_i) - x_i||^2 / (2 * sigma(f_dec_sigma2(z_i)))

For NB/ZINB the training loss is the standard negative log-likelihood: `recon_loss = -px.log_prob(x).sum(dim=-1).mean()`.

Adversarial loss per discrete covariate d (e.g., drug ID, cell line):

l^d_i := CrossEntropy(f^d_adv(z_basal_i), d_i)

Combined objective (paper): "minimize l_i - lambda * (l^d_i + sum_j l^c_{i,j})" where the encoder maximizes adversarial loss while the discriminator minimizes it via alternating updates.

KL term. Section 3 of the methods notes that "we implemented a deterministic autoencoder scheme, with extensions toward variational models and cost functions other than mean squared error also being straightforward." The released code uses the discrete reconstruction families above, not a VAE prior; there is no KL term in the canonical CPA objective.

Predict-time output. The user-facing `get_expression` returns the distribution's location/mean parameter:

```
if self.recon_loss == 'gauss':
    output_key = 'loc'
else:
    output_key = 'mu'
reconstruction = getattr(px, output_key)
```

So out of the box CPA returns means. To obtain samples for ConfPert's wrapper, we draw from the returned distribution object (`Normal(loc, scale)` or `NegativeBinomial(mu, theta)`) at inference, or repeatedly call generative with stochastic dropout/encoder noise. CPA also exposes per-cell variance for Gaussian, giving a built-in (Gaussian) uncertainty channel separate from any conformal procedure.

## Datasets / experiments

- sci-Plex 3 (Srivatsan et al. 2020): "188 compounds were tested in three cancer cell lines ... 290 thousand cells" across four dosages. CPA holds out unseen drug-dose pairs.
- Norman et al. 2019 CRISPRa genetic screen: "105 genes (i.e., single gene x, single gene y, and pair x + y) ... approximately 108,000 single-cells, where 131 are unique combination pairs." CPA predicts unseen single+single combinations.
- Kang et al. PBMC IFN-beta: "PBMCs from eight patients with Lupus ... treated with IFN-beta or control cells." Used as a cross-cell-type generalization benchmark.
- Cross-species/time-course datasets are also evaluated. There is no IPF / lung disease atlas application in the published paper despite some downstream uses in the wider lineage.

## Metrics

R^2 between predicted and observed mean expression and variance, computed both on all genes and on the top-K DE genes for the held-out condition: "We computed this on the entire gene expression vector, and on the top 50 DEGs exclusively for that condition." Specific reported numbers include: "CPA significantly outperformed scGen on variance prediction by 35.85% improvement" and the combination case "Panobinostat and Alvespimycin ... was predicted with an R2 of 0.81." For the sci-Plex OOD split, "we withheld cells exposed to the second to largest dose among all drugs" and for combinations "we held out the highest dose (10 micromolar) of the 36 drugs with the strongest effect ... Drug, dose, and cell line combinations in the OOD cases were removed from the train and test sets."

## What we steal for ConfPert

CPA is a prime wrappable predictor for ConfPert. It produces a per-cell parameterized distribution at predict time (Gaussian loc/scale or NB mu/theta), so we can draw S samples per (cell, perturbation) query and feed them to a conformal calibration layer to produce coverage-valid prediction sets or intervals on top-DE-gene expression. We adopt CPA's existing splits verbatim for direct comparability: sci-Plex unseen drug-dose, Norman unseen genetic combination, Kang cross-cell-type IFN-beta. On those splits CPA reports point R^2 metrics; ConfPert adds (i) marginal coverage, (ii) conditional coverage stratified by drug class and dose, (iii) interval width versus a heuristic Gaussian interval from CPA's own sigma head, (iv) coverage under covariate shift (held-out cell type) using weighted/shifted conformal methods.

## What we wrap

CPA is wrappable predictor #1 in our list. It is shipped as a standalone PyPI package `cpa-tools` that depends on scvi-tools internals (it uses the scvi-tools hyperparameter optimizer and module patterns). Install: `pip install cpa-tools` (stable, Colab-ready) or `pip install git+https://github.com/theislab/cpa` (latest). Requires PyTorch >= 1.13.1. The model class is `cpa.CPA`. Public pretrained checkpoints and preprocessed AnnData for sci-Plex, Norman, and Kang are distributed via the project Google Drive linked from the GitHub README. We need the conditional generative path that returns a distribution object (not just `get_expression` mean) to draw S Monte Carlo samples per query for conformal calibration.

## Failure modes / caveats

- Adversarial training instability: mode collapse and oscillation between encoder and discriminator. A weak discriminator leads to covariate leakage in z_basal, contaminating "counterfactual" predictions.
- Dose calibration: CPA uses a learned dose-response scaling on the perturbation embedding; extrapolation beyond training doses is unreliable and OOD performance reported in the paper is for held-out doses inside the trained dose range, not above it.
- Distributional miscalibration: the Gaussian head's variance is not a valid posterior predictive interval (no calibration guarantee). This is precisely the gap ConfPert closes.
- Library size / count statistics: NB head requires correct size factor handling; mishandling produces systematically biased mean predictions on lowly expressed genes.
- Combinatorial generalization is empirically partial: best results are on combinations whose constituents are well represented in training.

## Predict() output format - confirmed

By reading the released `cpa/_module.py`: the user-facing reconstruction returns the distribution mean (`loc` for Gaussian, `mu` for NB/ZINB). The generative pass internally constructs a full distribution object (`Normal`, `NegativeBinomial`, or `ZeroInflatedNegativeBinomial`). For ConfPert, samples are not returned by default but are one `.sample(sample_shape)` call away on the generative distribution. This is the integration point: ConfPert wraps CPA by calling `module.generative(...)` to obtain `px`, then drawing `px.sample((S,))` to produce S samples per (cell, perturbation) for conformal calibration.
